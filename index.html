I am a Ph.D. candidate working with Prof. M. K. (Peter) Yau. My main research interests are tropical cyclones (TCs) and mesoscale convective systems. The current project focuses on the eyewall replacement cycles of TCs.
I have been a weather enthusiast since my early ages because many types of severe weather (particularly TC) have frequently and strongly influenced my hometown.

Research highlight

My current research focuses on the eyewall replacement cycles (ERCs) of tropical cyclones. In particular, the project explores the underlying processes related to inner eyewall decay.

Multiple-eyewall structure

Multiple-eyewall structure is a frequently observed feature of intense TCs; and the most common form is double-eyewall. This process is about the formation of an outer (secondary) eyewall which takes place outside the existing (primary) eyewall. The figure on the left (source: Houze et al. 2010) shows a schematic diagram of a double-eyewall TC.



This is the real-time radar-satellite image of Hurricane Rita (2005). The inner and outer eyewalls are shown in deep red colour (source: Houze et al. 2006).

ERCs and the cut-off effect

Once the outer eyewall is formed, it is able to weaken the inner eyewall by intercepting and consuming the radially inward flow with abundant moisture and angular momentum from the environment. This is called as the cut-off effect and is briefly described by the schematic diagram on the left (source: Didlake et al. 2017). When the inner eyewall dissipated, we describe that the outer eyewall replaced the inner eyewall.

ERCs and the dynamical instability between the two eyewalls

The region of convective minimum and vorticity minimum between the two eyewalls is referred to as a moat. This local vorticity minimum makes the vortex susceptible to the barotropic instability across the moat (a.k.a. the type-2 instability). The figure on the left (from Lai et al. 2019) shows an azimuthal wavenumber-2 (elliptic) evolution associated with the type-2 instability in the simulated Hurricane Wilma (2005) of which the inner eyewall became elliptic before the decay.



Detailed budget analyses of absolute angular momentum (e.g. the figure on the left from Lai et al. 2020) revealed that the radial eddy transport of absolute angular momentum associated with the vorticity mixing of the type-2 instability can significantly weaken the inner eyewall. This effect of the type-2 instability can also work synergistically with the cut-off effect. See Lai et al. (2020) for more details.
